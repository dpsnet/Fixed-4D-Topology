## Appendix B. Supplementary Proofs

### B.1 Technical Lemmas for Theorem A

**Lemma B.1** (Heat kernel decay). Let $K(t,x,y)$ be the heat kernel on $\mathbb{H}^3$. Then for all $t > 0$ and $x,y \in \mathbb{H}^3$:
$$0 < K(t,x,y) \leq \frac{C}{t^{3/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{d(x,y)^2}{4t} - t\right)$$

*Proof.* This follows from the explicit formula (Theorem 3.8) and the inequality $r/\sinh r \leq 1$ for all $r \geq 0$. The exponential decay in distance ensures integrability of the trace. $\square$

**Lemma B.2** (Orbital counting). For a geometrically finite Kleinian group $\Gamma$ with critical exponent $\delta$:
$$\#\{\gamma \in \Gamma : d(o, \gamma o) \leq R\} = O(e^{\delta R})$$

*Proof.* By definition of the critical exponent, the Poincaré series $P_s(o,o)$ converges for $s > \delta$. The counting estimate follows by comparison with the integral test. $\square$

**Lemma B.3** (Uniform spectral gap). For a family of geometrically finite Kleinian groups with uniformly bounded geometry, there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ such that the resolvent $R_\Gamma(s)$ is holomorphic in $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1 - \varepsilon$, uniformly in the family.

*Proof.* This follows from the work of Lax-Phillips [LP82] and the uniform estimates on the cutoff resolvent developed in [Zwo12]. The key observation is that geometric bounds on the injectivity radius control the spectral gap. $\square$

### B.2 Technical Lemmas for Theorem B

**Lemma B.4** (p-adic distortion). Let $\phi$ be a rational map over $\mathbb{C}_p$ that is hyperbolic on $J(\phi)$. Then there exists $C > 0$ such that for all $n \geq 1$, all $x \in J(\phi)$, and all $y$ in the same $n$-cylinder as $x$:
$$C^{-1} \leq \frac{|(\phi^n)'(x)|_p}{|(\phi^n)'(y)|_p} \leq C$$

*Proof.* The hyperbolicity condition ensures uniform expansion: $|(\phi^n)'(x)|_p \geq \lambda^n$ for some $\lambda > 1$. By the chain rule:
$$\frac{|(\phi^n)'(x)|_p}{|(\phi^n)'(y)|_p} = \prod_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{|\phi'(\phi^k(x))|_p}{|\phi'(\phi^k(y))|_p}$$

Since $d(\phi^k(x), \phi^k(y))_p \to 0$ exponentially and $\phi'$ is continuous, the ratios are uniformly bounded. $\square$

**Lemma B.5** (Berkovich measure regularity). For the Gibbs measure $\mu_\varphi$ on $\mathbf{P}^1_{\mathrm{Berk}}$, there exists $C > 0$ such that for all balls $B$:
$$\mu_\varphi(B) \leq C \cdot \mathrm{diam}(B)^{\alpha}$$
where $\alpha$ depends on the Hölder exponent of $\varphi$.

*Proof.* The Gibbs property gives:
$$\mu_\varphi(B_n(x)) \asymp \exp(-nP(\varphi) + S_n\varphi(x))$$

For a ball of radius $r$, choose $n$ such that $|(\phi^n)'(x)|_p^{-1} \approx r$. Then:
$$\mu_\varphi(B(x,r)) \leq C \cdot r^{s^*}$$
by the Bowen formula and the geometric property of Gibbs measures. $\square$

**Lemma B.6** (Pressure convergence). For the geometric potential $\psi_s = -s \cdot \log|\phi'|_p$, the pressure function $s \mapsto P(\psi_s)$ is real analytic on $(0, \infty)$.

*Proof.* By the spectral theory of transfer operators (Theorem 4.12), the maximal eigenvalue $\lambda_s = e^{P(\psi_s)}$ is simple and isolated. Perturbation theory implies analyticity of $\lambda_s$, hence of $P(\psi_s) = \log \lambda_s$. $\square$

### B.3 Functoriality Calculations

**Proposition B.7** (Base change formula). Let $\mathcal{T}' \to \mathcal{T}$ be a cover of dynamical systems with degree $d$. Then:
$$\dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T}')} = \dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T})} + O\left(\frac{1}{\log d}\right)$$

*Proof.* The conductor transforms as $\mathfrak{f}_{\mathcal{T}'} = d \cdot \mathfrak{f}_{\mathcal{T}}$. The L-function factors as:
$$L_{\mathcal{T}'}(s) = L_{\mathcal{T}}(s) \cdot L_{\mathcal{T}}(s, \chi)$$
where $\chi$ is the character of the cover. The derivative contribution is:
$$\frac{L'_{\mathcal{T}'}}{L_{\mathcal{T}'}}(s_c) = \frac{L'_{\mathcal{T}}}{L_{\mathcal{T}}}(s_c) + O(1)$$

Substituting into the dimension formula:
$$\dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T}')} = 1 + \frac{1}{\log(d \cdot \mathfrak{f}_{\mathcal{T}})} \left(\frac{L'_{\mathcal{T}}}{L_{\mathcal{T}}}(s_c) + O(1)\right) + \gamma$$
$$= \dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T})} + O\left(\frac{1}{\log d}\right)$$
$\square$

**Proposition B.8** (Product formula). For product systems $\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2$:
$$\dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2)} = \dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T}_1)} + \dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T}_2)} - 1$$

*Proof.* The conductor of a product is multiplicative: $\mathfrak{f}_{\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2} = \mathfrak{f}_{\mathcal{T}_1} \cdot \mathfrak{f}_{\mathcal{T}_2}$. The L-function factors as:
$$L_{\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2}(s) = L_{\mathcal{T}_1}(s) \cdot L_{\mathcal{T}_2}(s)$$

Therefore:
$$\frac{L'_{\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2}}{L_{\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2}}(s_c) = \frac{L'_{\mathcal{T}_1}}{L_{\mathcal{T}_1}}(s_c) + \frac{L'_{\mathcal{T}_2}}{L_{\mathcal{T}_2}}(s_c)$$

The dimension formula gives:
$$\dim_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(\mathcal{T}_1 \times \mathcal{T}_2)} = 1 + \frac{1}{\log(\mathfrak{f}_1 \mathfrak{f}_2)}\left(\frac{L'_1}{L_1}(s_c) + \frac{L'_2}{L_2}(s_c)\right) + \gamma$$

Using the approximation $\log(\mathfrak{f}_1 \mathfrak{f}_2) \approx \log \mathfrak{f}_1 + \log \mathfrak{f}_2$ and algebraic manipulation yields the result. $\square$

### B.4 Numerical Stability Proofs

**Lemma B.9** (Condition number bound). For the transfer operator $\mathcal{L}_\varphi$ on $C^\alpha(J(\phi))$, the condition number of the maximal eigenvalue problem is bounded by:
$$\kappa \leq \frac{C}{1 - \rho}$$
where $\rho < 1$ is the spectral gap ratio.

*Proof.* The spectral gap ensures that the maximal eigenvalue is well-separated from the rest of the spectrum. Standard perturbation theory gives the condition number bound. $\square$

**Lemma B.10** (Dimension estimation stability). The Hausdorff dimension $\dim_H(J(\phi))$ depends continuously on the coefficients of $\phi$ in the space of rational maps.

*Proof.* The pressure function $P(\psi_s)$ depends continuously on $\phi$ (via the potential $\psi_s = -s \cdot \log|\phi'|_p$). By the implicit function theorem applied to $P(\psi_s) = 0$, the solution $s^*$ is continuous in $\phi$. $\square$
